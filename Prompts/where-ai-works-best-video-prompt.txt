🗣️ NARRATIVE ARC:
Hook: AI will take on almost anything you ask — writing, planning, summarizing, coding, drawing. But "can try" is not the same as "built for," and AI is far better at some kinds of work than others. Tell the course's own story: when the students built this course, AI coded every page and every interaction, earning an A+ for coding. It also wrote the first drafts of the lessons and earned a C-: points in the wrong order, explanations that missed the meaning, no feel for how a lesson should flow. That gap is the whole lesson in one example — code follows patterns and AI is a pattern-making machine, but strong lessons don't follow a set pattern. Then aim that one ability at real work and it becomes four distinct strengths. One: patterned transformation — same meaning, new shape. It recasts your input into something clearer or better structured: cleaning up messy notes, reformatting data, translating, turning an outline into prose. Two: generative variation — ten versions in ten seconds. It predicts likely pieces and there are many likely options, so it brainstorms angles, rewrites in a different tone, drafts common documents. Three: semantic compression and retrieval — it reads long documents, sees past the words to what they mean, and shrinks a 300-page rulebook to the rules you need or surfaces the one section that matters. Four: structured reasoning and synthesis — give it the facts, the constraints, and the goal, and it works through them: planning a project, debugging code, comparing options, critiquing a draft. Explain why this works: training gave AI exposure to more examples than any human could read in a lifetime — code, essays, arguments, conversations — so it's fluent in common formats; it has seen many versions of "this kind of thing" before. Then the limit that closes the lesson: exposure makes AI good at the COMMON version of a task, not necessarily the true, current, personal, or best version. That's exactly why your judgment still matters.
