🗣️ NARRATIVE ARC:
Hook: what's the magic behind ChatGPT, Claude, and every AI you've used? Math. You met these ideas by name in How an LLM Works — now see how each one actually works. 1654: Pascal and Fermat traded letters about gambling, and that correspondence is where standard probability is dated from. The math was clean: list every possible outcome and you can calculate the chance of each. One coin: two outcomes, heads is one of two — fifty percent. Two coins: four outcomes, both-heads is one of four — twenty-five. But standard probability only counts what you can see; it can't say how much to change your mind when fresh evidence arrives. Thomas Bayes worked that out. Someone peeks: the first coin landed heads. That rules out every tails-first outcome — cross them out and recount, and both-heads jumps from twenty-five percent to fifty. The evidence moved the probability. That update is conditional probability. The third idea turns both into language: autoregressive generation — your phone's keyboard suggesting the next word, except it never stops. After AI picks a word, it uses it to pick the next, then both to pick the third; every prediction depends on every word that came before. Watch all three on one rainy afternoon: standard probability gives the base rate — it rained forty of the last hundred May 21sts, forty percent. A clue arrives — humidity is ninety percent right now — and conditional probability sharpens it to sixty. Then the forecast is written one word at a time: given "It is going to," the model scores rain at seventy-one percent, picks it, and feeds it back in — no outside clue this time, because the words it already wrote are the clue. The real math goes further — linear algebra moves the numbers, calculus tunes the model in training — but two ideas from probability, plus the loop that turns them into language, are the foundation. Everything else is engineering.
