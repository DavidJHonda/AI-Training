🗣️ NARRATIVE ARC:
Hook: AI makes mistakes, and it uses the same confident voice whether the facts are right or wrong. So what do you do with an answer that might be wrong? Ignoring AI isn't an option — it's too powerful to leave on the shelf. You evaluate, in five steps. Read: weak users pass AI's output along without reading it; reading every word is the floor. Understand: you can't judge what you don't understand, and the fastest explainer is often the same AI — "explain that paragraph in simpler terms" costs one message. Validate: hold the answer against your own knowledge — a well-stocked brain is your fastest fact-checker. But watch its limit: "three gold medals at the 1932 Olympics in Athens" sounds fine, except the 1932 Games were in Los Angeles and the athlete doesn't exist. The more specific the detail, the more skeptical you should be — beyond what you know, "sounds fine" isn't validation. Decide, with two questions: what kind of task was this — facts need more checking, while drafts and brainstorms just get checked against what you asked for; and how much is riding on it — a movie pick, you're done; a college essay or health question, keep going. Dig deeper: ask for citations and click them — if you can't find a source, assume it doesn't exist. Make it argue the other side. Have it search the live web for anything recent. Don't let one true fact vouch for the rest. And take health, legal, or crisis questions to a real person. Then the relief: read, understand, validate takes seconds, and most chats never need the dig. Every evaluation ends in one of three moves: use it — most answers end here; fix it — name exactly what's wrong, which beats starting over; or walk away — wrong tool, or stakes too high. Close: most answers pass in seconds. The skill isn't checking everything — it's knowing which answers deserve the dig.
