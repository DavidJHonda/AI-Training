🗣️ NARRATIVE ARC:
Hook: you type real words to ChatGPT and it responds the same way. Underneath: text becomes tokens, and each token's vector carries its meaning. If only it were that easy. Two nuances in language break that idea — in both, a word's meaning isn't clear until you read the words around it. First, different meanings: please turn on the LIGHT — the suitcase is LIGHT enough to carry. Which meaning should AI use? Second, pronouns: the cat drank the milk because IT was thirsty — or because IT was fresh. Change the last word and IT points somewhere else: the cat, or the milk. Humans see the right meaning instantly. AI couldn't, because it used to read in order, one word at a time — the further it read, the more the early words faded. By the time it reaches IT, cat is ten words back, nearly gone. In 2017, eight Google researchers published Attention Is All You Need, introducing the Transformer — the architecture behind every modern LLM, and the T in ChatGPT. The Transformer reads your whole message at once — it ties IT to CAT no matter how far apart they sit, and sees that the CAT is TIRED. Reading at once is only the start — meaning takes two steps. Step one, attention: which words matter? Reading IT, the model weighs every word and leans hardest on CAT. Step two, transformation: update the meaning — IT's vector updates to mean CAT, not the mat, May, or the rainstorm. Now both questions get answered: attention links LIGHT to turn-on in one sentence, to carry in the other; transformation sets brightness in one, not-heavy in the other. Attention links IT to the cat when it's thirsty, to the milk when it's fresh; transformation locks each in. Beyond these examples — sarcasm, idioms, an "it" that points to nothing: every nuance resolves the same way, by weighing the words around it. That's why eight researchers dared such a bold title. Close: attention is all you need.
