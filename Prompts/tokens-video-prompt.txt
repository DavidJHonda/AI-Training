🗣️ NARRATIVE ARC:
Hook: when you type a question into ChatGPT, you ask in plain English and get plain English back — it feels effortless, because you think in language. A computer can't do that. Here's the fact underneath everything: computers work only with numbers; they don't read text at all. So before AI can read your question, all your text must become numbers. The obvious solution — give every English word its own number — falls apart fast: counting names, slang, typos, and code you'd need millions of numbers, and you still couldn't cover words nobody's invented yet. Software engineers found a smarter way: break language into reusable chunks. Take every word that starts with UN — unbelievable, unmatchable, untied: thousands of words reuse that one piece, so the vocabulary stores UN once. Before your words ever reach the model, a small ordinary program — no AI involved — breaks them into these chunks. The process is tokenization, and the chunks are tokens: a whole word, part of a word, punctuation, an emoji, even the space before a word. Each model knows a fixed set of them, its vocabulary, and these run large — ChatGPT's holds about two hundred thousand tokens, Gemini's about two hundred fifty-six thousand; Anthropic hasn't published Claude's. Each token gets a number, its token ID — an address in the vocabulary that tells the model which token, but says nothing about what it means. You see the word cat and instantly know: soft fur, whiskers, sits on your keyboard. AI sees 9246. Watch real splits: unbelievable breaks into three known parts; basketball into two; ChatGPT — brand names get split — into three; I-heart-AI into three, with a token that includes the leading space; and a long URL into seven known pieces. Once the vocabulary is built, the model uses that same fixed set every time it reads text. Close: tokenization turns your words into token IDs — addresses, not meanings.
