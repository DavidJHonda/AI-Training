🗣️ NARRATIVE ARC:
Hook: when Socrates asked his famous questions, he aimed them at people, and a person meets your question halfway — your teacher already knows what class you're in, and when a person doesn't understand, they ask a follow-up. AI does none of that: it starts from the words you typed and confidently answers whatever you gave it. So asking AI well takes one more skill: packaging the question for something that isn't a person. That's prompting, and the payoff is direct: the quality of your prompt controls the quality of the response. Every strong prompt starts life as a good question — open-minded, specific, on target, open-ended. Then add three moves. Move one: share your situation. AI only has what you hand over — who you are, who the work is for, what you're working on, and the material itself. Move two: describe the answer you want. The model fills in every blank you leave, so give the shape, the limits, an example to match, and who AI should be — a tutor, a coach, an editor. The lesson's caption example: the bare ask gets something corporate; adding "one sentence, no hashtags, sound like a senior wrote it, not the school account" gets the caption you wanted. Move three: one job at a time. Big work goes in steps so each part lands and you can check it before building on it — don't order a five-page paper in one breath; start with the thesis. Then the relief: you don't need all of this every time. A quick factual question: just ask. Real work: share your situation and describe the answer. A big project: add steps. The more the result matters, the more you bring. Close: entire classes exist on prompting; the secret is you don't need one. A prompt is a briefing, not magic words: share where you stand, describe what good looks like, give it one job at a time. That's the whole art.
