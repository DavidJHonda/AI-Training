🗣️ NARRATIVE ARC:
Hook: every conversation with AI starts with you asking something, and that moment decides more about what comes back than anything else. There are only two parts — the question and the answer — and technology keeps making answers cheaper. The research-assignment example: grandparents went to the library — card catalog, hunting the shelves, copying notes by hand — half a Saturday. Parents ran searches, judged which sites to trust, pasted the good parts — an hour or two. You: open an AI app, ask, done — seconds. Answers got cheap. That doesn't make humans less valuable; it moves where value lives. When anyone can get a fast answer, the edge goes to whoever asks the better question. That's one of the oldest skills there is: Socrates taught by asking; the scientific method starts with a question worth testing; Einstein supposedly said he'd spend fifty-five minutes of his hour finding the right question. Four qualities separate good questions from weak ones, and none mention AI. Open-minded: you haven't picked the answer in advance — a leading question is a request for backup, not research. Specific: it says enough about your situation — "how do I get better at sports" earns generic advice; naming that you're a point guard losing the ball under pressure earns drills that fix it. On target: it aims at the problem you're actually solving — energy drinks to stay awake in class is a perfect answer to the wrong problem when your sleep schedule is the issue. Open-ended: yes-or-no ends the conversation; open-ended starts it, leaving room for an answer you didn't expect. Close: these four worked in Socrates' Athens and work in a chat window; now that answers are instant, the question is the whole game. A better question doesn't make the answer true — it makes it focused, useful, and easier to check. The advantage goes to the person who frames the problem, sets the standard, and asks the next better question.
